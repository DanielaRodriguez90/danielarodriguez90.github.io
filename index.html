F is for Florida

How the state of Florida is failing to uphold its constitution.

Let's start from the beginning...

Article IX, section 1 of the Florida Constitution states the following regarding its commitment to funding kindergarten through grade 12 education in the state:

“The education of children is a fundamental value of the people of the State of Florida. It is, therefore, a paramount duty of the state to make adequate provision for the education of all children residing within its borders. Adequate provision shall be made by law for a uniform, efficient, safe, secure and high quality system of free public schools that allows students to obtain a high quality education…”

As Article IX states, it is the state’s duty to “make adequate provision for the education of all children '', yet as a parent of an elementary school student in Florida, it seems that the state is failing at times in this “paramount duty”.

The 2022-2023 Funding For Florida School Districts, found on Florida’s DOE website, states that sources of funds for school districts are broken down into three categories, state, local, and federal support. Though state funds are “provided primarily by legislative appropriations”, local support is provided “almost entirely from property taxes levied by Florida’s 67 counties, each of which constitutes a school district”. In the 2020-2021 school year, school districts in Florida received 48.74 percent of funding from local sources.

The Florida Education Finance Program (FEFP), enactd in 1973, established the state's policy on equilized funding. The FEFP was created to guarantee that each student in the Florida public education system has the "availability of programs and services appropriate to his or her educational needs that are substantially equal to those available to any similar student notwithstanding geographic differences and vayring local economic factors". Unlike progroms in other states, where financial support is provided based on the number of teachers or classrooms, the FEFP calculates financial support for public schools based on the "individual student participating in a particular educational program". Funds provided by the FEFP are generated by "multiplying the number of full-time equivalentt (FTE) students in each of the funded education programss by cost factors to obtain weighted FTE students". The weighted FTE is then multiplied by base student allocation (BSA) and district cost differential (DCD), this determines the base funding from state and local FEFP funds.

What does this all mean?

Overall, on paper Florida's formula for funding its public school system is one of the most equitable across the nation. The problem lies in the "large disparities in counties' tax bases, a local funding "loophole"", writes Holly Bullard for Florida Policy Institute. Though the FEFP accounts for programs lik Exceptional Student Education (ESE) and English Language Learners (ELL), it does not account for poverty.

The problem

FEFP dependence on property values has widen the funding gap among districts (counties) in the state, where high poverty areas are underfunded compared to low poverty areas.

How is the state performing?

In the Education Law Center's 2021 report, "Making the Grade: How fair is school funding in your state?", Florida is one of just two states that received an 'F' in each of the following "measures of fairness":

  • Funding Level- cost-adjusted, per-pupil revenue from state and local sources
  • Funding Distribution- The extent to which additional funds are distributed to districts with high student poverty levels
  • Funding Effort- Dollars allocated to PreK-12 public education as a percentage of the state's economic activity

    • The average national per-pupil funding level for the 2018-2019 school year was $15,487, Florida ranked 45th in this categeroy, on average the state spent $4,484 less. In contrast, states like Vermont, and New York, spent more than double per-pupil, with Vermont ranking 1st at $27,235.

    Cost-Adjusted Per-Pupil Funding Level by State Relative to National Average (2019)

    Perhaps the most outrageous of part of this study conducted by the ELC, is the funding distribution. Florida's funding level average was $11,003 per-pupil, yet when comparing, high poverty and low poverty areas, the state is funding low poverty areas at nearly $1,500 more per-pupil. This means, on average, high poverty areas, and therefore, public school students in these areas, are underfunded, not at all what the state constitution calls making "adequate provision for the education of all children".

    Funding Effort

    Funding Effort and State Capacity

    Florida again ranks among the worst states when it comes to funding effort with 2.55% Effort Index, the national average is 3.37%. The national average per capita GDP is $56,830, states like Vermont and Maine, similar to Florida, average approximately $10,000 less than than that, however, both Vermont and Maine scored at the top of this category with Effort Indexes above 4.0%.

    District performance

    District Grades

    The Florida public school system is made up of 67 districts, each district represents one county. Each year, the Florida Department of Education (DOE), releases both School and District grades for the previous year, under its Florida School Accountability Reports page on the DOE website.

    Miami-Dade County, the largest district in the state, and fourth largest in the nation, received an "A" for three consecutive years. Other districts like Desoto, on the other hand, have received grades no better than a "C" over that same span of time. How? As a parent, if I were to live in Desoto County, I would seriously consider moving to nearby Sarasota County, which has received an "A" grade as far back as 2010.
