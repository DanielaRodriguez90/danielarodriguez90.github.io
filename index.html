So, what exactly does the Florida Constitution say?

Article IX, section 1 of the Florida Constitution states the following regarding its commitment to funding kindergarten through grade 12 education in the state:

❝ The education of children is a fundamental value of the people of the State of Florida. It is, therefore, a paramount duty of the state to make adequate provision for the education of all children residing within its borders. Adequate provision shall be made by law for a uniform, efficient, safe, secure and high quality system of free public schools that allows students to obtain a high quality education…
❞

As Article IX states, it is the state’s duty to “make adequate provision for the education of all children '', yet it seems that the state is continuously failing in this “paramount duty”.

The 2022-2023 Funding For Florida School Districts, found on Florida’s DOE website, states that sources of funds for school districts are broken down into three categories, state, local, and federal support.

The Florida Education Finance Program (FEFP), enactd in 1973, established the state's policy on equilized funding. The FEFP was created to guarantee that each student in the Florida public education system has the "availability of programs and services appropriate to his or her educational needs that are substantially equal to those available to any similar student notwithstanding geographic differences and vayring local economic factors". Unlike progroms in other states, where financial support is provided based on the number of teachers or classrooms, the FEFP calculates financial support for public schools based on the "individual student participating in a particular educational program". Funds provided by the FEFP are generated by "multiplying the number of full-time equivalentt (FTE) students in each of the funded education programss by cost factors to obtain weighted FTE students". The weighted FTE is then multiplied by base student allocation (BSA) and district cost differential (DCD), this determines the base funding from state and local FEFP funds.

What does this all mean?

Overall, on paper Florida's formula for funding its public school system is one of the most equitable across the nation. The problem lies in the "large disparities in counties' tax bases, a local funding "loophole"", writes Holly Bullard for Florida Policy Institute. Though the FEFP accounts for programs lik Exceptional Student Education (ESE) and English Language Learners (ELL), it does not account for poverty. So, what's the problem? FEFP dependence on property values has widen the funding gap among districts (counties) in the state, where high poverty areas are underfunded compared to low poverty areas.


Compared to other states, Florida ranks among the worst in funding equity.

Funding Level

The average national per-pupil funding level for the 2018-2019 school year was $15,487, Florida ranked 45th in this categeroy, on average the state spent $4,484 less. In contrast, states like Vermont, and New York, spent more than double per-pupil, with Vermont ranking 1st at $27,235, this stat alone might be enough for any parent to up and move to Vermont.

Funding Distribution

Perhaps the most outrageous of part of this study conducted by the ELC, is the funding distribution. Florida's funding level average was $11,003 per-pupil, yet when comparing, high poverty and low poverty areas, the state is funding low poverty areas at nearly $1,500 more per-pupil. This means, on average, high poverty areas, and therefore, public school students in these areas, are underfunded, not at all what the state constitution calls making "adequate provision for the education of all children".

School Districts, like the state, underperform, and underdeliver.

The Florida public school system is made up of 67 districts, each district represents one county. Each year, the Florida Department of Education (DOE), releases both School and District grades for the previous year, under its Florida School Accountability Reports page on the DOE website. But what do these grades actually mean? Are "A" graded districts and schools really outperforming the others?

Miami-Dade County, the largest district in the state, and fourth largest in the nation, received an "A" for the last three years. Other districts like Desoto, on the other hand, have received grades no better than a "C" over that same span of time. How? As a parent, if I were to live in Desoto County, I would seriously consider moving to nearby Sarasota County, which has received an "A" grade as far back as 2010.

District Report Cards

Out of Florida's 67 districts, only 14 received an "A", 35 received a "B", and 18 received a "C" grade. The majority of the large population districts, like Miami-Dade and Broward, fall in the middle section between 40% and 60% of the student population considered economically disadvantaged. Many, of the counties that were graded as "C" districts, are smaller in population, with all but one district, having more than 50% of it's student population at an economic disadvantage.

School Performance, the pattern continues...

So, we have identified all the ways the state, and the districts are failing to uphold that "paramount duty" described before, and now, it's time to choose your child's school. A decision that may well affect how well your child performs academically, and what resources are available to them.

Maybe, there is no choice, you simply send your child to the school nearest your home. How confident are you in this school? Is it a Title I school? And, if it is, what does that mean?

Title I Schools

According to the article, "Explained: What Is Title I and How Is It Used to Fund Our Schools?", Title I is one of the federal funding streams that supplements how much money each state allocates for schools. The U.S. Department of Education describes the program as being created:

❝ to ensure economically disadvantaged children receive a fair, equitable, and high-quality education, by helping to close academic acheivement gaps.
❞

Title I schools, in general, are seen as underperforming schools. Most times, when parents hear a school is a "Title I school", they look for other schools to send their children to, but if Title I just means you get more federal funds, why are they perceived negatively? The answer is simple, they, in fact, are underperforming, and perhaps, more important, "Title I money received by schools for low-income students is very small, about 5%".

Miami-Dade County is the largest county in the state, it's home to more than 300,000 K-12 students. Miami-Dade's size allows us to see how tightly correlated being a Title I schools is to low performance in both reading and math, the district was funded a measly $11,357 in 2022.

In conclusion...

Despite having one of the most equitable funding formulas in the nation, the loophole on property taxes in the state of Florida, make it so that the state never really fulfills its potential, and thus fails in upholding Article IX of the state Constitution. The truth is, the state is funding lower poverty areas more than high poverty areas, which is turn breeds inequality. K-12 students in this state receive crumbs in funding compared to other states and Florida legislators have to yet to address the problem or provide more funding. The system is broken, teachers have been leaving the profession in record numbers since the pandemic, there's a bus driver shortage, all things directly attributed to the lack of school funding.

Methodology

Data was collected through the Florida Department of Education, the Education Law Center, among other sources.

It was important for me to show how failure started at the very top tier, the state, and trickled all the way down to individual schools. One of the more interesting pieces of information I came accross while conducting research on the Title I, was that all Title I means is that the school gets "more" federal funds, for the longest time, I just assumed a Title I school was a "bad" school.

Though the initial idea for the project was to focus on the property tax "loophole" and how this affected the funding for schools, I was not able to find the exact wording or section that made this loophole possible. As part of future research or projects on this topic, I would I approach it with more location based information, focusing on one county, and how funding differs by zipcode within that county.
